The 80-Hour Mandate: Are Work Requirements Moving Americans to Jobs or Just Off the Rolls?

As the 'One Big Beautiful Bill' marks its first year, data reveals a sharp decline in welfare enrollment—but the promised surge in employment remains elusive.

By The Dispatch 2026-03-23

When President Trump signed the "One Big Beautiful Bill" (OBBB) into law on July 4, 2025, it was heralded as the "greatest dignity-of-work initiative in history." Central to the legislation was a sweeping expansion of work requirements for the Supplemental Nutrition Assistance Program (SNAP) and Medicaid, mandating that "able-bodied" adults document at least 80 hours of work, training, or community service per month to maintain their benefits.

The administration’s logic was straightforward: by conditioning assistance on employment, the government would "break the cycle of dependency" and funnel millions of Americans back into the labor force. "We’re moving people from welfare to work, from the sidelines to the front lines," the President declared during a February rally. Now, nearly a year into implementation, early data from the Congressional Budget Office (CBO), the Bureau of Labor Statistics, and independent research groups provides a clearer picture of whether the mandate is fulfilling its promise or simply trimming the federal budget at the expense of the vulnerable.

The Great Enrollment Drop

By the primary metric of the administration—reducing "dependency" as defined by program enrollment—the OBBB has been undeniably effective. According to recent CBO analysis, SNAP participation has plummeted by approximately 2.6 million people since the expanded rules took effect in late 2025 [1]. The bill raised the upper age limit for work requirements from 54 to 64 and eliminated exemptions for veterans, homeless individuals, and former foster youth.

Medicaid has seen a similar contraction. While the national mandate for the ACA expansion population is not fully enforceable until 2027, several "pioneer states" that implemented requirements early in 2026 have reported significant coverage losses. Projections suggest that once fully implemented, upwards of 5.3 million Americans will lose Medicaid coverage due to the new requirements [2].

What the Data Shows: Employment vs. Paperwork

While the rolls are shrinking, the data suggests that most of those losing benefits are not doing so because they found new jobs. A 2026 study of Georgia’s "Pathways to Coverage" program—the first state to fully activate the 80-hour mandate—found no statistically significant increase in employment compared to neighboring states without the requirement [10].

Instead, researchers point to "administrative churn" as the primary driver of enrollment declines. The OBBB requires beneficiaries to submit monthly documentation of their hours, a process that many find difficult to navigate due to limited internet access, shifting work schedules, or health-related barriers that do not technically qualify for a "disability" exemption. In Nebraska, which began its implementation in early 2026, nearly 40% of those who lost benefits were later found to be working enough hours but had failed to clear the reporting hurdles [9].

The Economic Ripple Effect

The contraction in federal spending has also created unexpected headwinds for state economies. The Commonwealth Fund projects that the reduction in SNAP and Medicaid funding will lead to a net loss of over 1 million jobs nationwide by late 2026 [7].

The logic is circular: as millions lose food assistance, they spend less at grocery stores and local businesses. As Medicaid funding to hospitals declines, healthcare systems—particularly in rural areas—are forced to cut staff. Estimates suggest these combined factors could reduce the national GDP by $113 billion in 2026, a figure that rivals the federal budget savings for the same period [11].

The Full Picture: Who Is Left Behind?

The human cost of the OBBB’s "dignity of work" focus is most visible in the spike in food insecurity. National rates rose to 16% in late 2025, up from 12.7% at the start of the year [6]. Vulnerable groups who were previously shielded from work mandates, such as the homeless and veterans, have been hit particularly hard. Without a fixed address or stable phone access, many homeless beneficiaries find it impossible to meet the monthly reporting requirements, even if they are working day-labor jobs.

Conservative proponents of the bill argue that these "growing pains" are necessary to shift the national culture away from government reliance. They point to the $186 billion in projected SNAP savings as a critical step toward fiscal solvency. "We are asking able-bodied people to contribute to the society that supports them," a White House spokesperson stated in January. "That is not a punishment; it is a principle."

Conclusion

As the 80-hour mandate continues to roll out nationwide, the tension between fiscal goals and social outcomes is sharpening. While the OBBB has successfully "trimmed the rolls" and reduced federal outlays, the central promise of the legislation—a more robust and employed labor force—remains unverified by the data. For now, the "dignity of work" seems to be manifesting more as a reduction in the social safety net than as a ladder into the workforce.
