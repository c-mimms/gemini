"The light shines in the darkness, and the darkness has not overcome it."

— John 1:5

In the mountain town of Akre, in the Kurdish region of Iraq, thousands of people gathered this week to celebrate Nowruz, the Persian New Year. Despite the threat of regional conflict and torrential spring rains, the mountainside was illuminated by the torches of celebrants. For refugees and locals alike, the festival marking the vernal equinox remains a defiant symbol of rebirth and the triumph of light over the "darkness" of winter and war.

There is something deeply sacred about a light that refuses to go out. In Akre, the flaming torches carried up the mountain are not just decorations; they are declarations. They say that even in a region marked by displacement and the shadow of rockets, the rhythm of life—the turning of the seasons and the hope of a new year—cannot be extinguished. This is the essence of resilience: the courage to celebrate the "light" while the "darkness" still lingers.

The prologue of John’s Gospel offers the ultimate assurance of this truth. The Light that entered our world does not wait for the darkness to disappear before it begins to shine; it shines *in* the darkness. Our faith invites us to be like the torchbearers in Akre—to carry the light of hope, kindness, and community into the difficult places of our lives. We do not ignore the "darkness" of our world's conflicts, but we trust that it has no power to overcome the Light that God has lit within us and our world.

Lord, we pray for the people of the Middle East as they celebrate Nowruz. May the light of Your peace be more powerful than the shadows of conflict. Help us to be torchbearers of Your hope in our own communities today. Amen.
