Mineral Processing Emissions: A 2024 Air Quality Assessment

2024 Emissions Overview

This analysis examines air pollutant data from the Georgia Environmental Protection Division (EPD) for the year 2024. The study focuses on the "Big Three" pollutants — Particulate Matter (PM10), Nitrogen Oxides (NOx), and Sulfur Dioxide (SO2) — emitted during the intensive drying, calcining, and milling processes central to Georgia's mineral industry.

  • Kaolin Hegemony: The kaolin sector (KaMin, Thiele, Imerys) remains the largest contributor to industrial particulate emissions in central Georgia.
  • Fuller's Earth Concentrations: Southwest Georgia's attapulgite (fuller's earth) operations show significant ammonia and particulate outputs.
  • Process Intensity: Emissions are primarily driven by the combustion of fossil fuels in massive rotary kilns and spray dryers.

Sector-Specific Emissions Profiles

Analysis of the ga_epd_emissions_2024.csv dataset reveals distinct "emissions signatures" for the different mineral sectors, reflecting the unique processing requirements of each commodity.

Kaolin Sector High SO2 and Particulates Kaolin processing involves high-temperature calcining to produce "hard" kaolin, which releases significant SO2 (up to 83 tons per plant) and fine particulates from spray dryers.
Fuller's Earth High Ammonia & Particulates The Southwest Georgia attapulgite plants exhibit massive ammonia emissions (over 1,400 tons at the BASF Attapulgus site), likely related to catalyst manufacturing and specific mineral treatment steps.
Industrial Sand Low Emission Intensity Sand and gravel operations, such as those run by Standard Sand or Arcilla, show negligible emissions compared to the clay-based industries due to simpler physical washing and sizing processes.

Analytical Observations

1. Particulate Matter: The Primary Challenge

Particulate matter (PM10 and PM2.5) remains the most significant air quality challenge for Georgia's mining industry. At the KaMin Gordon plant, nearly 240 tons of particulates were emitted in 2024, despite extensive baghouse and scrubber infrastructure. These emissions are largely fugitive dust from milling and dry-handling of processed clays.

2. The "Ammonia Peak" in Decatur County

The 1,471-ton ammonia emission from the BASF Attapulgus operation is an outlier in the mineral sector. While attapulgite (fuller's earth) is mined at this site, the facility also functions as a major catalyst production hub. The ammonia is likely a chemical byproduct of the catalyst synthesis process rather than the mining activity itself, demonstrating the intersection of mining and chemical manufacturing.

3. Fuel-Related NOx and SO2

Nitrogen Oxides (NOx) and Sulfur Dioxide (SO2) emissions are closely tied to the fuel source used for high-temperature dryers. Facilities like KaMin's Edgar and Gordon plants show elevated SO2 levels, suggesting a reliance on coal or fuel oil for certain calcining processes, whereas other facilities may have transitioned more fully to natural gas.

Data Methodology Note: Data extracted from the 2024 Georgia EPD National Emissions Inventory (NEI) submission. "PM10 Primary" includes both filterable and condensible particulate matter. Facilities were selected based on their primary SIC/NAICS codes related to mineral mining and clay processing.
