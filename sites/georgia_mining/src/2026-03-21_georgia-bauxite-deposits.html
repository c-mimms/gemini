Georgia Bauxite: The First American Source

In 1888, a resident of Floyd County, Georgia, discovered a strange, reddish-brown rock on his farm that would forever change the American aluminum industry. This discovery marked the first identification of bauxite in the United States, leading to the establishment of the Hermitage mining district and sparking a mineral rush that predated the major discoveries in Arkansas and Alabama.

Primary Analysis Results

  • Georgia was the first U.S. state to produce bauxite (1888), with initial shipments originating from the Hermitage district.
  • The USGS Mineral Resources Data System (MRDS) catalogs 66 significant bauxite deposits across two distinct geological provinces in Georgia.
  • Current production is largely focused on refractory-grade bauxite and high-alumina clays in the Andersonville district of the Coastal Plain.

Geological Provinces of Georgia Bauxite

Bauxite in Georgia occurs in two primary geological settings, each with distinct mineralogical characteristics and depositional histories.

Valley and Ridge (Northwest GA)

Located in Bartow, Floyd, and Chattooga counties. These deposits occur as pocket-like bodies in residual clays derived from the Knox Group carbonates. They were the primary source of early production.

  • Major Districts: Hermitage, Summerville, Cave Spring.
  • Status: Mostly historical past producers.

Coastal Plain (Central/SW GA)

Associated with the Paleocene-Eocene Nanafalia Formation and Wilcox Group. These deposits are typically lens-shaped and integrated with high-grade kaolin beds.

  • Major Districts: Andersonville, Springvale, Wrens.
  • Status: Continued interest for industrial/refractory use.

Economic Significance & 2026 Outlook

While Georgia no longer serves as a primary source of metallurgical-grade bauxite for aluminum smelting (which is now dominated by imports from Australia, Guinea, and Brazil), its deposits remain economically vital for high-temperature refractory materials and industrial chemicals.

2026 Commodity Status
Strategic
Aluminum remains a critical mineral for national defense and aerospace.
Primary End Use (GA)
Refractory
High-alumina clays from the Andersonville district are used in high-heat industrial bricks.
