Georgia's Industrial Clays: A Global Powerhouse

Georgia remains a global leader in the production of industrial clays, particularly kaolin and fuller's earth (attapulgite). According to the latest USGS 2026 State Rankings, Georgia ranks 12th in the United States for non-fuel mineral production value, with an estimated annual output of $2.75 billion. Industrial clays are the cornerstone of this economic engine, driving a significant portion of the state's 2.46% share of the national mineral total.

The Kaolin Belt: "White Gold" of the Fall Line

Kaolin, often referred to as "white gold," is a white, alumina-rich silicate clay mineral (kaolinite) formed from the weathering of feldspar. In Georgia, these deposits are concentrated along the Fall Line—the geological boundary between the Piedmont and the Coastal Plain. The deposits found in the Dry Branch Formation and Nanafalia Formation are of world-class purity and scale.

Fuller's Earth and the Attapulgite District

Southwest Georgia, specifically Thomas County, is part of the world-famous Meigs-Attapulgus-Quincy district. This region produces attapulgite, a rare needle-like silicate mineral known for its incredible absorbent properties. Unlike common fuller's earth, attapulgite remains stable in saltwater, making it essential for offshore drilling and specialized industrial filters.

Industrial Clay Analysis Summary

  • Georgia's Kaolin production is estimated to grow to 4.8 million metric tons in 2025, driven by global demand in specialized coatings.
  • The state accounts for the vast majority of US kaolin output, reinforcing its role as the "Kaolin Capital of the World."
  • Attapulgite production in the Meigs-Attapulgus district provides a unique commodity with specialized rheological properties not found in standard fuller's earth.
  • The stratigraphic transition along the Fall Line provides the ideal geochemical environment for high-purity kaolinite formation.
