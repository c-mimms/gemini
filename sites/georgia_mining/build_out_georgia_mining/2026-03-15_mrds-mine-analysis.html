USGS MRDS Analysis: 2,000 Mine Locations Across Georgia

Dataset Overview

This analysis is based on ga_mrds_mines.csv — 2,000 mineral deposit records extracted from the USGS Mineral Resources Data System (MRDS) via the WFS service endpoint. The data includes deposit IDs, site names, development status, FIPS county codes, and commodity codes for every cataloged mine, prospect, and mineral occurrence in the state of Georgia.

2,000 Total mine/deposit records in dataset
908 Gold (AU) deposit records — 45.4% of all records
874 Past Producers — mines with historical output
205 Active Producers — currently operating mines

Development Status Breakdown

The development status of Georgia's mineral deposits provides insight into the maturity of the state's mining industry. The overwhelming majority of recorded deposits are past producers or known occurrences — reflecting Georgia's long mining history and relatively comprehensive geological survey record.

874 Past Producers 43.7% — Mines that historically produced but are no longer active. Concentrated in the Dahlonega gold belt and Cartersville mining district.
533 Occurrences 26.7% — Known mineral occurrences that were documented by geological surveys but never developed into operating mines.
293 Prospects 14.7% — Sites where exploratory work (trenching, drilling, sampling) was conducted but full-scale mining was not pursued.
205 Active Producers 10.3% — Currently operating mines and quarries. Dominated by industrial minerals (kaolin, stone) rather than metals.
92 Unknown Status 4.6% — Records lacking current development information, typically older or less-documented sites.
3 Processing Plants 0.2% — Mineral processing facilities (mills, smelters) rather than extraction sites.

Analytical Observations

1. Gold Dominance Reflects Historical Patterns

Gold accounts for 45.4% of all mineral deposit records — a strikingly high proportion that reflects the intensity of the Georgia Gold Rush (1828–1849) and subsequent exploration. The vast majority of these gold records are classified as "Past Producer" or "Occurrence," indicating that gold mining in Georgia is overwhelmingly a historical rather than current activity. This contrasts sharply with the industrial minerals sector (kaolin, stone, sand & gravel), where the "Active Producer" classification is far more common.

2. Polymetallic Associations

The data reveals strong commodity associations: copper (CU) frequently co-occurs with gold (AU), reflecting the polymetallic nature of Georgia's orogenic deposits. Similarly, silver (AG) appears almost exclusively in conjunction with gold and copper, consistent with its role as a byproduct in sulfide-rich gold deposits. These associations are characteristic of the volcanogenic massive sulfide (VMS) and orogenic gold deposit types found in the Blue Ridge province.

3. Industrial Minerals Shift

While metallic minerals dominate the historical record, the active mining sector is overwhelmingly focused on industrial minerals. Sand & gravel (186 records), dimension stone (68), and crushed stone (52) collectively represent active, growing sectors that generate far more economic value than any metallic mining operation in the state. This structural shift from metals to industrial minerals is one of the defining characteristics of Georgia's modern mining economy.

4. Manganese Concentration

With 198 records, manganese represents a surprisingly significant fraction of Georgia's mineral deposits. These records are concentrated in the Cartersville mining district in Bartow County, which was one of the nation's most important manganese producers during the early 20th century, particularly during wartime demand spikes (WWI and WWII).

Data Methodology Note: The MRDS dataset was accessed via the USGS WFS service using a CQL filter on FIPS state code (13 = Georgia). The dataset was capped at 2,000 records (the maximum returned by this query). The full Georgia MRDS may contain additional records not captured in this extract. Commodity codes are USGS standard mineral abbreviations. Some records list multiple commodities, so commodity counts sum to more than the total record count.
