The Room Where It Happened: ENIAC and the Dawn of General-Purpose Computing

Accession Number: MIMMS-1946-001
Inauguration: February 15, 1946
Weight: 30 tons
Components: 17,468 vacuum tubes, 7,200 crystal diodes, 1,500 relays

In the basement of the Moore School of Electrical Engineering at the University of Pennsylvania, a monster was born. It wasn't made of flesh and bone, but of steel, copper, and glass. The ENIAC (Electronic Numerical Integrator and Computer) was the first programmable, electronic, general-purpose digital computer. Conceived by John Mauchly and J. Presper Eckert during World War II, it was initially designed to calculate artillery firing tables for the United States Army's Ballistic Research Laboratory. However, its completion in 1945 meant it would serve a postwar world, performing calculations for everything from weather prediction to the design of the hydrogen bomb.

Unlike modern computers that operate on binary (0 and 1), ENIAC was a decimal machine. It performed calculations in base-10, mimicking the way humans count. This made its architecture incredibly complex; it required twenty accumulators, each capable of storing a ten-digit number. To achieve electronic speeds, it relied on vacuum tubes. These tubes were notorious for their heat and fragility. Legend has it that the lights of Philadelphia would dim when ENIAC was switched on, though this is likely apocryphal. What is true, however, is that at least one tube failed every few days, requiring a specialized team of technicians to locate and replace the culprit among the nearly 18,000 glowing glass cylinders.

"The ENIAC was a son of a bitch to program, but it was the first time we could see the results of our logic in real-time. We weren't just turning cranks anymore; we were directing the flow of electrons."

While Eckert and Mauchly were the architects, the actual programming was done by a group of six women: Kay McNulty, Betty Jennings, Marlyn Wescoff, Ruth Lichterman, Elizabeth Bilas, and Jean Bartik. In 1946, "programmer" wasn't a job title yet. These women were hired as "computers" (a human role) and tasked with translating complex mathematical equations into the physical patches and switch-settings required by ENIAC. Programming ENIAC involved physically plugging in cables and setting three-position switches across 40 different panels. It was a task that required deep logical thinking and an intimate understanding of the machine's hardware—a far cry from the high-level languages of today.

The ENIAC represents the pivotal transition from mechanical calculation to electronic computation. Occupying 1,800 square feet and consuming 150 kW of electricity, it could perform 5,000 additions per second—thousands of times faster than any mechanical calculator of its era. It proved that electronic computing was not only possible but essential for the complexity of the modern age.

Modern Connection: The Smartphone in Your Pocket

A modern smartphone processor contains billions of transistors and can perform trillions of operations per second. To equal the computing power of a single iPhone 15, you would need an ENIAC the size of several city blocks, consuming the power of a small nuclear plant. We stand on the shoulders of the vacuum tubes that once flickered in Philadelphia.

ENIAC Milestone Timeline

1943
Construction begins at the Moore School under Project PX.
1945
ENIAC is completed and performs its first test run on the Los Alamos hydrogen bomb calculations.
1946
Public unveiling at the University of Pennsylvania. It is hailed as a "Giant Brain."
1955
ENIAC is finally decommissioned after nearly a decade of continuous service.
