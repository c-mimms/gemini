The ENIAC: Six Women and the Secret of the First Electronic Computer

In the chilly, high-ceilinged rooms of the University of Pennsylvania’s Moore School of Electrical Engineering in 1946, a monster was born. It was a beast of glass, copper, and heat, stretching 80 feet along the walls and weighing a staggering 30 tons. It hummed with the energy of 18,000 vacuum tubes, each one a fragile potential point of failure. This was the ENIAC—the Electronic Numerical Integrator and Computer—the first general-purpose electronic digital computer. But while the world marveled at the "Giant Brain" that could calculate ballistic trajectories in seconds, a secret remained hidden in plain sight: the machine didn't just "think" on its own. It was programmed, and the people doing the programming were six brilliant women whose names would be nearly forgotten for decades.

The Necessity of War

The ENIAC was born out of the desperate needs of World War II. The U.S. Army’s Ballistic Research Laboratory (BRL) was struggling to produce the thousands of firing tables required for new artillery pieces and bombs. These tables, which told soldiers at what angle to fire a gun under various atmospheric conditions, required complex differential equations to be solved by hand. This task was performed by hundreds of "computers"—human beings, mostly women with degrees in mathematics, who worked around the clock with mechanical calculators.

John Mauchly, a physicist, and J. Presper Eckert, a brilliant young engineer, proposed a radical solution: an electronic machine that could do the work of thousands of human computers. The Army, desperate for results, funded the project under the code name "Project PX." While Eckert and Mauchly focused on the unprecedented engineering challenge of keeping 18,000 vacuum tubes from burning out, the logic of how to actually *use* the machine fell to a different group.

The ENIAC 6: Programming the Unprogrammable

In 1945, six of the best human computers at the Moore School were selected to become the ENIAC’s first operators: Kathleen "Kay" McNulty, Frances "Betty" Jennings, Marlyn Wescoff, Ruth Lichterman, Frances "Betty" Snyder, and Jean Bartik. At the time, "programming" as a concept didn't exist. There were no manuals, no programming languages, and certainly no operating systems. The Army categorized their work as "sub-professional" labor, assuming that the real genius lay in the hardware design.

"We were expected to study the ENIAC's wiring diagrams to understand how the machine worked and then figure out how to program it. There were no manuals. We had to work it out from the circuit diagrams."
— Jean Bartik

To program the ENIAC, these women had to physically configure the machine. This wasn't a matter of typing code; it was a grueling physical and mental marathon. They moved from panel to panel, flipping thousands of switches and plugging in hundreds of heavy black patch cables (similar to those used in a telephone switchboard). They had to understand every pulse and gate within the machine’s architecture, mapping the flow of electrons to the steps of a mathematical algorithm.

A Masterpiece of Logic

One of the most significant breakthroughs came from Betty Snyder. While the ENIAC was designed to be a parallel processor, Snyder realized that for complex problems, they needed a way to control the sequence of operations. She developed what would later be known as a "subroutine" and implemented the machine's first "breakpoint"—a way to stop the computation at a specific point to check the results. Her logic was so sound that it laid the groundwork for the future of software architecture.

When the ENIAC was finally unveiled to the public on February 15, 1946, it was a sensation. It calculated a trajectory that had previously taken a human 30 hours in just 20 seconds. The press hailed the machine as a "Giant Brain." In the official photographs, the six women were often visible, standing by the panels, but they were rarely named. Many journalists assumed they were "refrigerator models" hired to make the machine look more appealing. It would take nearly 50 years for their contributions to be fully recognized and for them to be inducted into the Women in Technology International Hall of Fame.

The Legacy of the Giant Brain

The ENIAC was more than just a fast calculator; it was the proof of concept that launched the computer age. It paved the way for the EDVAC, the UNIVAC, and the stored-program computers that followed. But perhaps its most enduring legacy is the birth of software engineering as a discipline. The ENIAC 6 proved that the "soft" side of computing—the logic, the algorithms, and the user interface—was just as critical as the "hard" silicon and glass that contained it.

Today, as we carry computers in our pockets that are millions of times more powerful than the ENIAC, it is worth remembering the heat of those 18,000 vacuum tubes and the six women who stood before the panels, patch cables in hand, dreaming of a world that could be calculated into existence.

Technical Specifications: ENIAC (1946)

Manufacturer: University of Pennsylvania / U.S. Army

Primary Components: 17,468 vacuum tubes, 7,200 crystal diodes, 1,500 relays, 70,000 resistors, 10,000 capacitors

Weight: 27 tons (30 short tons)

Power Consumption: 150 kW

Speed: 5,000 additions per second (roughly 0.1 MHz)

Memory: 20 accumulators (each holding a 10-digit decimal number)
