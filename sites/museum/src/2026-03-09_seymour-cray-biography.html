Seymour Cray: Architect of the Supercomputing Age

Known as the "Hermit of Chippewa Falls," Seymour Cray was more than an engineer; he was an architect of speed. His philosophy was singular: build the fastest computer in the world, then do it again. This timeline follows his life through the physical milestones of his legendary machines.

1960

The CDC 1604

After leaving Univac, Cray's first major success at the newly formed Control Data Corporation was the 1604. It was one of the first commercially successful fully-transistorized computers, proving that vacuum tubes were a relic of the past.

Manufacturer: Control Data Corporation
Speed: ~100,000 operations per second
1964

The CDC 6600: The World's First Supercomputer

With the 6600, Cray achieved a 10x performance jump over existing machines. It featured a radical freon-cooling system and a distributed architecture that allowed it to process 3 million floating-point operations per second (MFLOPS), leaving IBM in the dust.

"It seems like the 6600 is the fastest computer in the world, and it will be for some time." — Thomas J. Watson Jr., IBM CEO (Internal Memo)
1976

The Cray-1

After founding Cray Research, he delivered the iconic C-shaped Cray-1. The unique design minimized wire lengths to maximize speed. It was the first computer to successfully implement vector processing, hitting 80 MFLOPS.

Signature Feature: "C" Shape for wire optimization
Weight: 5.5 Tons
1985

The Cray-2: "The Bubbles"

Cray's pursuit of speed led to the liquid-cooled Cray-2. To handle the immense heat, the entire machine was submerged in Fluorinert, a colorless, odorless liquid. The "waterfall" effect of the cooling system earned it the nickname "The Bubbles."

Cooling: Liquid Immersion (Fluorinert)
Clock Speed: 4.1 Nanoseconds
